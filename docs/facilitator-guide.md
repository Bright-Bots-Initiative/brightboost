# BrightBoost Pathways: Cyber Launch Facilitator Guide

## 6-Week Cybersecurity Curriculum — Facilitator Edition

---

## Introduction: Who This Guide Is For

You do not need a cybersecurity background to run this program. You need to show up, care about the people in the room, and follow this guide. The platform does the teaching. Your job is everything the platform cannot do: noticing when someone is lost, keeping energy up, making connections between what learners see on screen and what it means for their lives.

Your role is presence, accountability, and connection. You are not lecturing. You are not grading. You are creating a space where young people can focus, ask questions, and start seeing themselves in a career field that pays well and does not require a four-year degree to enter. When a learner finishes a module, you acknowledge it. When someone falls behind, you check in. When the room gets quiet, you ask a question.

This guide is organized by week. Read the section for the upcoming session five minutes before it starts. Each section tells you what learners are doing on the platform, what you should be doing in the room, what concepts matter, what to watch for, and what questions to ask. There is a checklist at the end of each week. Use it.

## Program Overview

BrightBoost Pathways: Cyber Launch is a six-week introductory cybersecurity program for young people ages 14 to 17. Learners work through interactive modules on the BrightBoost platform covering cybersecurity fundamentals, digital safety, networking, threat detection, career exploration, and a capstone project. The curriculum is mapped to the ISC2 Certified in Cybersecurity (CC) exam domains so that learners who complete the program are prepared to attempt an industry-recognized certification at no cost.

The program is designed for community organizations, reentry programs, youth workforce programs, and after-school settings. It does not assume learners have prior tech experience. It meets them where they are, builds foundational knowledge, and connects that knowledge to real career pathways.

### Explorer (14-15) vs Launch (16-17)

Both age bands complete the same six-week curriculum. The difference is pacing and depth. Explorer learners (ages 14-15) get slightly simplified language in their modules and more scaffolded activities. Launch learners (ages 16-17) see the same concepts with more technical detail and are expected to write longer responses in the capstone. In the room, this means Explorer learners may need more check-ins and encouragement, while Launch learners can often work more independently. If you have a mixed room, pair older learners with younger ones during discussion time.

### ISC2 CC Certification

The ISC2 Certified in Cybersecurity (CC) is an entry-level cybersecurity certification recognized across the industry. It proves a person understands security fundamentals. It does not require prior work experience. ISC2 offers a free exam voucher, which means learners can take the certification exam at no cost.

**How learners claim their free exam voucher:**

1. Go to [isc2.org/certifications/cc](https://www.isc2.org/certifications/cc)
2. Click "Get Started" and create a free ISC2 account
3. After creating the account, navigate to the CC certification page
4. Select the free exam voucher option (ISC2 provides this as part of their One Million Certified in Cybersecurity initiative)
5. Register for a Pearson VUE exam appointment using the voucher code
6. Choose a testing center or online proctored exam
7. Take the exam: 100 questions, 2 hours, passing score 700 out of 1000

Help learners create their ISC2 accounts during Week 5 so they are ready to schedule the exam after the program ends.

## Cohort Setup Checklist

1. **Log in** to the BrightBoost platform with your facilitator account
2. **Go to the facilitator dashboard** — click "Dashboard" in the top navigation
3. **Create a new cohort** — click "Create Cohort," name it (e.g., "Spring 2026 Cyber Launch — Eastside"), select the Cyber Launch curriculum, and choose the start date
4. **Copy the join code** — the system generates a 6-character code when the cohort is created
5. **Share the join code with learners** — write it on a whiteboard, print it on a handout, or text it. Learners enter this code when they create their accounts to join your cohort
6. **Verify band enrollment** — after learners join, check the cohort roster in your dashboard. Each learner should show as "Explorer" (14-15) or "Launch" (16-17) based on their age. If a learner is in the wrong band, click their name and update their age band
7. **If a learner does not have a device** — the platform works on any device with a browser. If your site has loaner laptops or tablets, assign one. If not, pair learners to share a device. Two learners per device is workable. Three is too many

## Week 1 — Orientation + Cyber Foundations

**ISC2 Domain: Security Principles (26% of exam)**

### What Learners Are Doing

Learners complete the Cyber Foundations module: five interactive slides covering what cybersecurity is, who works in it, what the threats look like, and why the field is growing. The module ends with a short knowledge check.

### Facilitator Job

**Before:** Read this section. Write the WiFi password and join code on the board. Test the platform on your own device to make sure it loads.

**During:** Help learners log in and join the cohort. Walk the room. When learners finish the module, ask them which slide surprised them most. Do not rush anyone. Some learners will finish in 15 minutes, others in 30.

**After:** Check your dashboard to confirm all learners show module completion. Note anyone who did not finish — follow up next session.

### Key Concepts

- **CIA Triad:** Confidentiality (keep secrets secret), Integrity (keep data accurate), Availability (keep systems running). This is the foundation of all cybersecurity thinking
- **Threat landscape:** The range of dangers — hackers, malware, phishing, insider threats
- **Security analyst role:** The entry-level job in cybersecurity. Monitor systems, respond to alerts, investigate incidents
- **Demand:** There are over 470,000 open cybersecurity jobs in the US right now. The field cannot fill positions fast enough

### Discussion Prompts

1. "Have you or someone you know ever been hacked, scammed, or had an account stolen? What happened?"
2. "What surprised you about the career information in the module?"
3. "If you were protecting a school's computer network, what is the first thing you would worry about?"

### Watch For

- Learners who cannot log in — help them reset passwords or re-enter the cohort code
- Learners who seem intimidated by the word "cybersecurity" — normalize it, remind them no prior knowledge is expected
- Learners who finish fast and look bored — ask them to help someone else or explore the career section

### Real-World Connection

Entry-level cybersecurity roles (Security Analyst, SOC Analyst) pay $55,000 to $75,000 depending on location. Many employers accept certifications in place of degrees. The ISC2 CC is one of those certifications, and it is free to attempt.

### Checklist

- [ ] All learners logged in and joined cohort
- [ ] All learners started Cyber Foundations module
- [ ] At least one group discussion happened
- [ ] Dashboard checked for completion status
- [ ] Noted any learners who need follow-up

## Week 2 — Digital Safety Sim

**ISC2 Domain: Security Principles + Access Controls (26% + 22%)**

### What Learners Are Doing

Five interactive scenarios that put learners in realistic situations: spotting a phishing email, creating strong passwords, recognizing social engineering, staying safe on public WiFi, and responding to a data breach notification.

### Facilitator Job

**Before:** Skim the five scenario titles so you know what learners will encounter. Have a personal example ready — a phishing text you received, a time you reused a password.

**During:** Walk the room. When learners hit the phishing scenario, pause the group and ask if anyone has seen something similar. Let learners work at their own pace but encourage them to read each scenario fully before choosing.

**After:** Check dashboard for scenario completion counts. Each learner should have completed all five.

### Key Concepts

- **Phishing red flags:** Misspelled sender domains, urgency language ("act now"), suspicious links, requests for passwords or payment
- **Password hygiene:** Long passwords beat complex ones. Use a passphrase. Never reuse passwords across sites. Use a password manager
- **Social engineering:** Manipulating people instead of hacking machines. Pretexting, baiting, tailgating
- **Two-factor authentication (2FA):** A second proof of identity beyond a password — usually a code sent to your phone

### Discussion Prompts

1. "Which scenario felt most realistic to you? Why?"
2. "Have you ever clicked on something and immediately regretted it?"
3. "Why do you think attackers go after people instead of trying to break through firewalls?"

### Watch For

- Learners who rush through scenarios without reading — ask them to explain their choice
- Learners who get every answer wrong — no shame, use it as a teaching moment about how convincing these attacks are
- Conversations about personal experiences with scams — validate them, connect to the content

### Real-World Connection

According to Verizon's Data Breach Investigations Report, 74% of all data breaches involve a human element — someone clicking a bad link, using a weak password, or being tricked by social engineering. The most expensive security tools in the world cannot fix human error. That is why this training matters.

### Checklist

- [ ] All learners completed five scenarios
- [ ] Group discussion covered at least phishing and passwords
- [ ] Dashboard shows completion
- [ ] Learners who missed Week 1 caught up on Cyber Foundations

## Week 3 — Network Basics

**ISC2 Domain: Network Security (24% of exam)**

### What Learners Are Doing

Learning how the internet actually works: packets, protocols, DNS, HTTP vs HTTPS, firewalls. The module includes a packet-tracing activity where learners follow a data packet from their device to a web server and back.

### Facilitator Job

**Before:** Read the key concepts below. You do not need to understand networking deeply. Know enough to say "DNS is like a phone book for the internet" and "HTTPS means the connection is encrypted."

**During:** The packet-tracing activity is the most complex thing learners have done so far. Walk the room more actively. Expect questions. If you do not know the answer, say "Let's look it up together" and use the module's glossary.

**After:** Check completion. The packet-tracing activity has a completion marker separate from the slides — make sure both show green.

### Key Concepts

- **IP address:** A unique number assigned to every device on a network, like a street address for your computer
- **DNS (Domain Name System):** Translates human-readable names (google.com) into IP addresses. The phone book of the internet
- **Firewall:** A security barrier that controls what traffic can enter or leave a network
- **Encryption:** Scrambling data so only authorized parties can read it
- **HTTPS:** The secure version of HTTP. The "S" means encrypted. Look for the padlock icon in browsers

### Discussion Prompts

1. "When you type a website into your browser, what do you think actually happens before the page loads?"
2. "Why would someone want to encrypt their internet traffic?"
3. "If you were building a firewall for a school, what would you allow through and what would you block?"

### Watch For

- Learners who get stuck on the packet-tracing activity — pair them with someone who finished
- Confusion between hardware and software concepts — keep it simple, use analogies
- Disengagement from learners who think "this is too technical" — remind them that understanding networks is what separates cybersecurity professionals from everyone else

### Real-World Connection

Network engineers and network security specialists are among the most in-demand IT roles. Starting salaries range from $55,000 to $80,000. Every organization with a computer network needs someone who understands how it works and how to protect it.

### Checklist

- [ ] All learners completed Network Basics slides
- [ ] All learners completed packet-tracing activity
- [ ] At least one discussion prompt used
- [ ] Dashboard checked
- [ ] Struggling learners identified for extra support next week

## Week 4 — Threat Detective

**ISC2 Domain: Security Operations (18% of exam)**

### What Learners Are Doing

Analyzing simulated log files to investigate security incidents. Learners look for patterns — failed login attempts (brute force), unusual traffic spikes (DDoS), suspicious file access. They then write a short incident report describing what they found, what they think happened, and what should be done.

### Facilitator Job

**Before:** Read the key concepts. Understand that a log file is just a list of events with timestamps. You do not need to read logs yourself.

**During:** Pair learners for the investigation phase. Two sets of eyes catch more than one. Walk the room and ask pairs what they are noticing. When learners write their incident reports, encourage clear, simple language — "write it like you are explaining to your boss what happened."

**After:** Have two or three pairs share their findings with the group. Compare what different pairs caught. Check dashboard for report submissions.

### Key Concepts

- **Log files:** Records of everything that happens on a system — logins, file access, errors, network connections. Every event gets a timestamp
- **Brute force attack:** Trying thousands of password combinations until one works. Shows up in logs as many failed login attempts from the same source
- **DDoS (Distributed Denial of Service):** Flooding a system with traffic to crash it. Shows up as a sudden massive spike in requests
- **Incident response lifecycle:** Identify the problem, contain it, fix it, recover, and learn from it

### Discussion Prompts

1. "What was the hardest part of reading the logs? What made it click?"
2. "If you found a real security incident at your job, who would you tell first and what would you say?"
3. "Why is writing a clear incident report just as important as finding the problem?"

### Watch For

- Learners who feel overwhelmed by the log data — remind them to look for patterns, not read every line
- Incident reports that are too vague ("something bad happened") — push for specifics: what, when, how they know
- Learners who are energized by this activity — this is a sign they might enjoy SOC analyst work

### Real-World Connection

SOC (Security Operations Center) analysts are the people who monitor systems and respond to threats in real time. Starting salary is $50,000 to $70,000. They use tools called SIEMs (Security Information and Event Management) that collect and analyze logs automatically. What learners did manually today, a SIEM does at scale.

### Checklist

- [ ] All learners paired for investigation
- [ ] All learners submitted incident report
- [ ] Group debrief completed
- [ ] Dashboard checked for completion
- [ ] Learners who showed strong interest noted for career conversation

## Week 5 — Career Exploration

**ISC2 Domain: All domains (career awareness)**

### What Learners Are Doing

Exploring the Cyber Career Map — six cybersecurity roles with descriptions, salary ranges, required skills, and certification pathways. Learners also get a link to Cisco NetAcad for additional free training. By the end, each learner identifies their top two roles of interest.

### Facilitator Job

**Before:** Look at the six roles on the Career Map so you can speak to them. Have the ISC2 account creation steps ready (see Program Overview above).

**During:** After learners explore the Career Map, go around the room and ask each person to share their top two roles and why. This is not a quiz — there are no wrong answers. Help learners create their ISC2 accounts during this session.

**After:** Confirm each learner submitted their top two roles in the platform. Confirm ISC2 account creation. Begin talking about next week's capstone.

### Key Concepts

- **Career pathways:** Cybersecurity has many specializations. Not every role is the same
- **Certification landscape:** CC is the starting point. CompTIA Security+ is the next step. Specialized certs come later
- **Bootcamp vs degree:** Both are valid paths. Bootcamps are faster (12-24 weeks). Degrees provide broader education. Many employers care more about certifications and demonstrated skill than where you went to school

### Discussion Prompts

1. "Which role surprised you the most? Which one did you not expect to exist?"
2. "Do you know anyone who works in tech or cybersecurity? What do they do?"
3. "If you could start any of these careers in two years, which one would you pick and why?"

### Watch For

- Learners who say "none of these are for me" — dig deeper, ask what they do care about, and find the connection
- Learners who are excited but worried about cost — emphasize free certification, free training, and employer-paid education
- Learners who want to move faster — point them to Cisco NetAcad, TryHackMe, or CyberSeek.org for self-study

### Real-World Connection

CyberSeek.org (cyberseek.org) is a free tool built by NICE and CompTIA that shows cybersecurity job openings by region, career pathways, and salary data. Have learners look up their local area. Seeing real job postings in their city makes it tangible.

### Checklist

- [ ] All learners explored Career Map and submitted top two roles
- [ ] All learners created ISC2 accounts
- [ ] Group sharing of career interests completed
- [ ] Cisco NetAcad link shared
- [ ] Capstone project introduced — learners know what to expect next week

## Week 6 — Capstone + Showcase

**ISC2 Domain: All domains (applied)**

### What Learners Are Doing

Building a security plan for a real or fictional business. Learners pick a business type (restaurant, clinic, school, small online store), identify three to five security risks, recommend protections for each risk, and write a one-page executive summary explaining their plan in plain language. This pulls together everything from the previous five weeks.

### Facilitator Job

**Before:** Decide if you want to invite community members, partner staff, or other cohorts to watch presentations. Even two or three guests make it feel like an event. Prepare a simple agenda: welcome, presentations, questions, closing.

**During:** Give learners the first half of the session to finish their plans. Use the second half for presentations. Each learner or pair gets three to five minutes. Ask one question after each presentation. Clap. Make it feel real.

**After:** Celebrate completion. Share next steps: scheduling the ISC2 CC exam, continuing with Cisco NetAcad or TryHackMe, connecting with local workforce programs. Export the cohort progress report from the dashboard for your records and your funder.

### Key Concepts

- **Risk assessment:** Identifying what could go wrong and how likely it is
- **Security controls:** The specific protections you put in place — firewalls, training, encryption, backups, access controls
- **Executive communication:** Explaining technical recommendations to non-technical people. This is a critical professional skill

### Discussion Prompts

1. "What was the hardest part of building your security plan?"
2. "If a business owner asked you 'Why should I spend money on cybersecurity?' what would you say?"
3. "What is one thing you know now that you did not know six weeks ago?"

### Watch For

- Learners who are nervous about presenting — offer to let them present just to you first, or present as a pair
- Security plans that are too vague — push for specific, actionable recommendations
- The energy shift when learners realize they built something real — name it, reinforce it

### Real-World Connection

Security consultants do exactly this work: assess a business's risks and recommend protections. Junior security consultants earn $60,000 to $85,000. The ability to explain technical concepts in plain language is what separates good security professionals from great ones.

### Checklist

- [ ] All learners completed security plan
- [ ] Presentations happened (even informal ones)
- [ ] ISC2 exam scheduling discussed
- [ ] Next steps shared (NetAcad, TryHackMe, workforce programs)
- [ ] Cohort progress report exported from dashboard
- [ ] Celebrate

## Troubleshooting & FAQ

**Learner can't log in**
Check that they are using the correct email address. Try resetting the password from the login screen. If they joined with a cohort code, confirm the code is correct and the cohort is still active. Try clearing the browser cache or using an incognito window.

**Module won't load**
Refresh the page. Check the internet connection. Try a different browser (Chrome and Firefox work best). If the problem persists on one device, try another device. Report persistent issues to BrightBoost support.

**Progress didn't save**
Refresh the page and check again. Make sure the learner clicked the "Complete" button at the end of the module — progress does not auto-save on every page. If data is still missing, have the learner redo the final step of the module.

**Learner wants to move faster**
Let them. If they finish a module early, they can move to the next week's content or explore additional resources (Cisco NetAcad, TryHackMe, CyberSeek). Check in on the quality of their work — speed without understanding does not help.

**Learner is disengaged**
Start with a question, not a correction. "What part of this feels least interesting to you?" or "If you could learn any tech skill, what would it be?" Connect the content to something they care about. If they are interested in gaming, talk about game server security. If they are interested in music, talk about protecting intellectual property. Meet them where they are.

**Sharing progress with a funder or partner**
Go to the facilitator dashboard, select your cohort, and click "Export Progress Report." This generates a summary showing each learner's completion status by module. You can also screenshot the progress view for quick updates.

### Career Roles — Plain Descriptions

**Security Analyst** — Monitors computer systems for threats. Investigates alerts. Writes reports. Entry-level. $55K-$75K.

**Penetration Tester (Pen Tester)** — Gets paid to hack into systems to find weaknesses before real attackers do. Requires deeper technical skills. $70K-$100K.

**Incident Responder** — The first person called when a security breach happens. Contains the damage, investigates the cause, leads recovery. $65K-$90K.

**Security Engineer** — Builds and maintains the security tools and systems that protect an organization. More technical than analyst, more building than monitoring. $80K-$120K.

**GRC Analyst (Governance, Risk, and Compliance)** — Makes sure the organization follows security laws, regulations, and policies. More writing and policy work, less hands-on-keyboard. $60K-$90K.

**CISO (Chief Information Security Officer)** — The executive in charge of all cybersecurity for an organization. Senior leadership role. $150K-$300K+. This is the long-term goal, not the starting point.
